### 1. Introduction to Course Outcomes (COs)

A **Course Outcome (CO)** is a statement that describes what a student should be able to do after successfully completing a course. COs are measurable, observable, and specific to the course content.

For this course (Probability and Statistics), the COs include:
- **CO1:** Apply statistical methods to analyze and interpret data using measures of central tendency, dispersion, moments, skewness, kurtosis, correlation, and regression.
- (Other COs will be introduced in later modules.)

COs are written using **action verbs** from Bloom's Taxonomy to ensure they are measurable.

**Program Outcomes (POs)** are broader statements that describe the knowledge, skills, and behaviors students acquire by the end of a program (e.g., a B.Tech degree). POs are defined by accrediting bodies like NBA (National Board of Accreditation).

### 3. Bloom's Taxonomy and Knowledge Levels

Bloom's Taxonomy classifies cognitive skills into six levels, from lower-order thinking to higher-order thinking. Each level uses specific action verbs that help us write measurable COs.

| Level | Category | Description | Example Verbs | Example in Statistics |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| L1 | Remember | Recall facts and basic concepts | Define, list, name, recall, identify | "Define mean, median, and mode." |
| L2 | Understand | Explain ideas or concepts | Explain, interpret, describe, summarize | "Explain the difference between correlation and regression." |
| L3 | Apply | Use information in new situations | Calculate, solve, demonstrate, use | "Calculate the standard deviation of a given data set." |
| L4 | Analyze | Draw connections among ideas | Compare, contrast, examine, differentiate | "Compare skewness values to determine the shape of a distribution." |
| L5 | Evaluate | Justify a decision or stance | Evaluate, argue, defend, assess | "Evaluate which measure of central tendency is most appropriate for a given data set." |
| L6 | Create | Produce new or original work | Design, develop, formulate, construct | "Design a statistical experiment to test a hypothesis and interpret the results." |

#### Detailed Breakdown with Statistical Examples

**L1 -- Remember**
- Definition: Retrieving relevant knowledge from long-term memory.
- Statistics example: "List the three measures of central tendency."
- Test question: What is the formula for the mean of grouped data?

**L2 -- Understand**
- Definition: Constructing meaning from instructional messages.
- Statistics example: "Interpret the value of the correlation coefficient r = 0.85."
- Test question: Explain what it means when a data set has positive skewness.

**L3 -- Apply**
- Definition: Carrying out or using a procedure in a given situation.
- Statistics example: "Compute the regression line for a given bivariate data set and predict y for a given x."
- Test question: The marks of 10 students in two subjects are given. Find Karl Pearson's coefficient of correlation.

**L4 -- Analyze**
- Definition: Breaking material into parts and detecting relationships.
- Statistics example: "Compare the kurtosis of two data distributions and determine which is more peaked."
- Test question: Analyze the bivariate frequency table to determine if there is a linear relationship between variables.

**L5 -- Evaluate**
- Definition: Making judgments based on criteria and standards.
- Statistics example: "Determine whether the mean or median better represents the central tendency of a skewed data set."
- Test question: Evaluate the appropriateness of using a linear regression model for a given data set.

**L6 -- Create**
- Definition: Putting elements together to form a coherent whole.
- Statistics example: "Develop a statistical model to forecast sales based on historical data."
- Test question: Design a study to investigate the relationship between hours of study and exam performance, including data collection and analysis methods.

### 4. Mapping COs to POs

Each CO is mapped to one or more POs with a strength indicator:
- **3** = Strongly mapped
- **2** = Moderately mapped
- **1** = Weakly mapped
- **-** = Not mapped

Example mapping for CO1 of this course:

| CO | PO1 | PO2 | PO3 | PO4 | PO5 | PO6-PO12 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CO1 | 3 | 3 | 2 | 2 | 1 | - |

**Rationale:**
- **PO1 (Engineering Knowledge):** CO1 requires applying mathematical formulas (mean, variance, correlation), which is engineering knowledge. Strength: 3.
- **PO2 (Problem Analysis):** CO1 requires identifying data patterns and solving statistical problems. Strength: 3.
- **PO3 (Design/Development):** CO1 contributes to designing solutions involving data interpretation. Strength: 2.
- **PO4 (Conduct Investigations):** CO1 is used in research data analysis. Strength: 2.
- **PO5 (Modern Tool Usage):** Software tools (Excel, Python) can be used. Strength: 1.
